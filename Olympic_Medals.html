Spain is dreaming big for the Paris 2024 Olympic Games.

No one is hiding that the medal target is to beat the 22 medals won in Barcelona’s 1992 Olympics, the best Olympic Games of the country’s history.

But is Spain on the right track?

Gold, Silver or Bronze?

To reach the 22 medals they won in 1992, they now need to win an average of 2.3 medals per day.

The current count of silver and bronze medals is equal or higher than that achieved in Barcelona, but it’s the gold medals that are the missing. Can Spain make up for its gold medal shortcomings?

Milestones

With 382 athletes, Spain presents the second-largest contingent in their history of the Games (the record number of 430 was in Barcelona ’92). And for the first time, there are more women (192) than men (190) in the delegation.

We still have a week to go and I will keep you updated here!

In a first step, Germany hopes to stop the downward trend in the medals table.

The official goal is to stay in the top 10 like three years ago in Tokyo and to bounce back from that worst result since their reunification.

But is Germany on the right track?

Gold, Silver or Bronze?

They need to win 3.6 medals on average per day to match their 37 medals from 2020.

While the total medal target seems far away, with the number of gold medals they’ve already won, Germany is well on their way to matching their last position.

Are the going to reach the 10 times gold from Tokyo?

France’s Olympic dream is evolving from host to champion.

Their medal mission, officially communicated by Emmanuel Macron, is to return to the top 5 as in Atlanta 1996, where they have so far obtained their gold medal record.

But is France on the right track?

Gold, Silver or Bronze?

The 1996 team has set the bar high with 37 medals, and they’re aiming to reach that number once again. To achieve this, they’ll need to win -0.7 medals on average per day.

Can they set a new record?
